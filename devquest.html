The Hypothesis

Overview & Academic Scope

Traditional educational games ship with hand-authored difficulty curves: fixed enemy health values, predetermined puzzle timers, and static resource budgets. These curves are calibrated for an average learner and systematically fail students at the extremes — frustrating high-performers and overwhelming those with limited prior knowledge or neurodivergent processing styles.

This dissertation proposes and evaluates an alternative: a Reinforcement Learning agent, trained in simulation using Unity ML-Agents and PPO, that continuously observes player behavioural signals and adjusts in-game parameters in real time. By treating difficulty as a dynamic policy rather than a fixed schedule, the system aims to keep every player inside their individual flow state — maximising engagement and knowledge retention simultaneously.

Research Focus

The study concentrated on the following execution pillars:

  • Agent Training: Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) agents were trained inside isolated Unity simulation environments, receiving reward signals derived from player performance telemetry — penalising premature deaths and rewarding sustained engagement above a target threshold.
  • Adaptive Systems: At runtime, a lightweight telemetry loop sampled player health, kill rate, and time-per-objective every 10 seconds. The trained policy network consumed these metrics and emitted discrete difficulty adjustments — modifying enemy aggression, spawn density, and puzzle timer windows — without breaking game flow.

Core Technology Stack

  • Unity ML-Agents: PyTorch-backed reinforcement learning toolkit for game agents.
  • C# Architecture: Observer patterns for data telemetry collection.
  • Python Scripting: Data analysis and trajectory graphing.
  • Academic Formatting: IEEE/Harvard standard technical writing.

The Results

Findings & Conclusion

What Was Measured

  • Session Engagement: Uninterrupted play duration and voluntary quit events were tracked across both the adaptive-difficulty group and a static-curve control group during structured observation windows.
  • Perceived Difficulty: Post-session Likert surveys captured how participants rated the balance of challenge — comparing the subjective distribution of responses between both groups.
  • Task Completion: The proportion of learners reaching each curriculum milestone within the allotted time was compared across conditions to evaluate whether adaptive tuning reduced friction without trivialising content.

Key Takeaways

  • RL Viability at Small Scale: The study evaluated whether a compact, locally-trained PPO policy can operate at interactive frame rates inside Unity without a cloud inference backend — addressing a key barrier for indie and educational studio adoption.
  • Accessibility as Architecture: Dynamic difficulty scaling was framed as a structural accessibility feature rather than an optional setting, avoiding the stigma of manual difficulty selection while accommodating a wider range of cognitive profiles.
  • Future Directions: The methodology identifies gaze-tracking, biometric telemetry, and longer longitudinal studies as viable extensions that would improve ecological validity beyond the scope of this prototype evaluation.
